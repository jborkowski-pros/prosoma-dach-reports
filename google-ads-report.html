1. Executive Summary

  • Optimization setting changed multiple times. Location #1's selective optimization was changed multiple times: Google Play (Jul–Aug 2025) → Firebase (Oct–Dec 2025) → Google Play again (Feb–Mar 2026). Location #2 has always been Firebase-only, creating a natural experiment.
  • TK contract loss is the dominant external factor. The contract with TK (Techniker Krankenkasse) — one of Germany's largest health insurers — ended in December 2025. From January 2026, TK-insured patients can no longer register or activate vouchers. This dramatically reduced the eligible patient pool and is likely the primary driver of the registration→voucher rate drop (15–16% → 9–10%), not seasonality or optimization settings.
  • Small signal, overwhelmed by confounders. After controlling for the TK contract loss and seasonality, the gap between campaigns is ~5 percentage points wider when Location #1 uses Google Play vs when both use Firebase — but sample sizes are small and the TK eligibility change makes before/after comparisons fundamentally unreliable.

8. Confounding Factors

The following factors make this analysis observational rather than conclusive. A causal claim cannot be made from this data.

  1. TK contract termination (December 2025). The contract with Techniker Krankenkasse (TK) — one of Germany's largest statutory health insurers — ended in December 2025. From January 2026, patients insured by TK can no longer register in the app or activate vouchers. This dramatically reduced the eligible patient pool and is likely the single largest factor driving the registration→voucher rate decline from ~15–16% to ~9–10%. Any before/after comparison spanning this date is fundamentally compromised by this eligibility change.
  2. Different geographies. Location #1 targets 5 "high-converting" German states, Location #2 targets the rest. These populations may respond differently to campaigns regardless of optimization setting.
  3. Seasonality. Q4 (Oct–Dec) may show better conversion rates than Q1 (Jan–Feb), though the TK contract loss makes it difficult to separate true seasonal effects from the eligibility reduction.
  4. Budget reallocation. In Jan 2026, budget shifted dramatically: Location #1 went from ~61K to 112K PLN/month while Location #2 dropped from ~91K to 61K. Higher spend can cause diminishing returns and inflate CPAs.
  5. Sample sizes. voucherActivated counts are small (67–265 per period). Observed differences of 5–6 percentage points may not be statistically significant.
  6. Multiple simultaneous changes. The optimization setting change in Jan 2026 coincided with the budget shift AND the TK contract loss, making it impossible to isolate the effect of any single variable.
  7. Unequal time periods. The Firebase phase spans 3 clean months (Oct–Dec 2025), while the Google Play phase has only 1 clean month (Feb 2026). Jul–Aug 2025 was a ramp-up period with very low spend.
  8. No true control. A proper A/B test would change one variable while keeping everything else constant. That did not happen here — geography, budget, time period, TK eligibility, and optimization setting all differ between the comparison groups.

10. Conclusion

What the data shows

  • Location #1's selective optimization was changed at least twice (Sep 2025 and Jan 2026), with transitions visible in the primary conversion ratio data.
  • Both campaigns show the same decline in registration→voucher rates from Q4 2025 to Q1 2026, regardless of the optimization setting.
  • The TK contract termination (Dec 2025) coincides exactly with the voucher rate drop, and is the most likely primary driver — it removed a significant portion of eligible patients from the market.
  • The performance gap between campaigns is smaller when both use Firebase (~9%) vs when #1 uses Google Play (~14%), but this signal is weak and confounded.
  • Google Play downloads is classified as secondary (non-primary) at the account level by default.

What the data does NOT show

  • Whether Firebase or Google Play optimization causes better outcomes (too many confounding variables, especially the TK contract loss).
  • Whether the previous "failed test" observation was actually a failure, a seasonal coincidence, or partially masked by TK eligibility changes.
  • What would happen if Location #1 switched to Firebase with current budget levels, geo targeting, and the reduced (post-TK) patient pool.
  • How much of the voucher rate decline is attributable to TK loss vs seasonality vs optimization settings — these effects cannot be separated with available data.

What would be needed for a definitive answer

  • A clean A/B test: same geo, same budget, same time period, only the optimization setting differs.
  • Or a longitudinal comparison: Run Location #1 on Firebase for 2–3 months (the current change from Mar 6) and compare to the Feb 2026 baseline, controlling for Location #2's trend as a seasonality proxy. Crucially, this comparison must account for the post-TK patient pool being permanently smaller than pre-TK — absolute voucher numbers from before Dec 2025 are not directly comparable to after.
  • TK-adjusted baseline: If data is available on what share of voucher activations came from TK-insured patients, this could be used to normalize pre/post comparisons and isolate the optimization setting effect.
